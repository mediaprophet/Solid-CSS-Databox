# CSS – Databox Code of Conduct

## Purpose

CSS – Databox exists to support human agency, accountable organisations and trustworthy information exchange. The
project expects its contributors, maintainers, demonstrators and project-supported deployments to respect human
rights and to avoid causing, enabling or concealing adverse human-rights impacts.

This policy incorporates the Solid [Code of Conduct](https://github.com/solid/process/blob/main/code-of-conduct.md)
as a community-participation baseline and adds requirements specific to Databox technology, sensitive personal
information, automated agents and organisation-to-person power relationships.

The commitment is informed by the United Nations
[Universal Declaration of Human Rights](https://www.ohchr.org/en/UDHR/Documents/UDHR_Translations/eng.pdf) and the
[Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights](https://www.ohchr.org/documents/publications/guidingprinciplesbusinesshr_en.pdf),
including their emphasis on dignity, non-discrimination, respect for rights and access to effective remedy.

## Our commitment

Project participants commit to creating an environment and technology practice that supports:

- inherent human dignity and the equal and inalienable rights of every person;
- equality, accessibility and freedom from unlawful or harmful discrimination;
- privacy, bodily and mental integrity, identity, family and freedom of association;
- autonomy, informed choice and meaningful control over personal disclosures;
- freedom of thought, expression, belief and peaceful participation;
- access to essential services without exploitative data demands;
- recognition as a person, procedural fairness, explanation, contestability and effective remedy;
- particular care for children and people or communities at heightened risk of vulnerability or marginalisation; and
- accountability for adverse impacts, including correction, mitigation, remedy and prevention of recurrence.

Human rights are interconnected. A claimed benefit to one right or group does not automatically justify avoidable
harm to another. Applicable law, jurisdiction, context and the views of affected people must be considered.

## Expected conduct

Everyone participating in CSS – Databox project spaces is expected to:

- communicate respectfully, including when discussing contested legal, political, cultural or technical questions;
- welcome people of different backgrounds, identities, abilities, disciplines and levels of technical experience;
- identify foreseeable harms, affected groups, power imbalances and less harmful alternatives;
- minimize collection, disclosure, linkage, inference, retention and replication of personal information;
- use synthetic or properly authorised data in demonstrations, tests, examples and bug reports;
- distinguish an organisation's assertion from an independently verified fact;
- preserve a person's ability to access, understand, dispute and seek correction of information about them;
- provide meaningful human review for decisions that may significantly affect rights, services, safety or livelihood;
- document uncertainty, provenance, limitations, conflicts of interest and unresolved review requirements;
- make interfaces accessible and avoid excluding people who cannot use a particular identity, device or channel;
- respond constructively to evidence of harm and support proportionate remediation; and
- protect people who raise concerns from retaliation or loss of service.

## Unacceptable conduct and unsupported uses

The project will not endorse, promote or provide project-run support for contributions or deployments intended to:

- facilitate slavery, trafficking, forced labour, torture, cruel treatment or other serious abuse;
- enable unlawful surveillance, stalking, persecution, collective punishment or political repression;
- infer or expose sensitive characteristics without a legitimate, necessary and proportionate purpose;
- discriminate unlawfully or use proxies to reproduce prohibited discrimination;
- deny housing, health care, welfare, education, employment, justice or another significant service solely through an
  opaque or unreviewable automated decision;
- coerce consent or make access to an essential service conditional on unrelated or excessive data disclosure;
- suppress a person's correction, complaint, evidence, appeal or access to an effective remedy;
- expose a consumer's independent Pod, other organisational relationships or private diary to an organisation;
- retaliate against a person for exercising privacy, correction, access, association or other rights;
- create false credentials, misleading evidence, defamatory demonstrations presented as fact or deceptive audit
  trails;
- describe a system as legally compliant, unbiased, safe or human-rights preserving without appropriate evidence and
  qualified review; or
- use project spaces for harassment, intimidation, threats, sexualised attention, doxing or publication of private
  information.

Security research, documentation of abuse and carefully controlled adversarial testing are permitted when they are
authorised, proportionate, use the minimum necessary data and follow responsible disclosure practices.

## Human-rights evaluation for agents and deployments

Automated agents can help identify relevant considerations, but they must not be treated as authorities on a person's
rights or as substitutes for legal, policy, clinical, social-work or community expertise.

An agent or deployment making a rights-relevant recommendation should:

1. establish the jurisdiction, organisational role, affected people, decision purpose and material context;
2. identify potentially applicable rights, duties, prohibitions, exceptions and routes to remedy;
3. distinguish authoritative instruments and official sources from derived ontologies, mappings and commentary;
4. retain source provenance, version, retrieval time and integrity information;
5. expose uncertainty, conflicting rights, missing facts and alternative interpretations;
6. test for disproportionate effects, accessibility barriers and impacts on vulnerable or marginalised groups;
7. avoid a final automated determination where the outcome may significantly affect a person;
8. provide a qualified human reviewer with the source material and an intelligible explanation;
9. allow the affected person to provide context, challenge facts and seek review or remedy; and
10. record the decision, accountable actor, evidence considered, limitations and follow-up duties.

Agents must not send private Databox records, consumer identifiers, credentials or relationship metadata to an
external evaluation service unless there is explicit authority, a defined purpose, appropriate security and a lawful
data-handling arrangement. Local, pinned and integrity-checked resources should be preferred for sensitive work.

## Web Civics namespace service

[ns.webcivics.net](https://ns.webcivics.net/) publishes a semantic registry designed for human-centric systems,
including RDF vocabularies, SHACL shapes and machine-readable representations of human-rights instruments. These
resources may support agents and reviewers in identifying and structuring human-rights considerations.

Use of that service or its resources does not by itself establish which law or instrument applies, resolve conflicts
between rights, prove the accuracy of a derived representation or certify compliance. Implementations should pin the
resource version or digest used, retain provenance, compare material propositions with official sources and require
appropriately qualified human review before relying on an evaluation in a significant decision.

The Web Civics namespace must not become an undeclared telemetry or disclosure channel. Loading a public vocabulary
is different from transmitting a person's private facts for remote analysis.

## Human-rights review in project work

A change requires explicit human-rights consideration when it affects identity, eligibility, assurance, surveillance,
profiling, sensitive records, corrections, referrals, service priority, automated decisions, law-enforcement access,
children, people experiencing vulnerability or the ability to seek remedy.

Pull requests and release reviews for such changes should record:

- the intended benefit and affected rights;
- people and groups who may experience adverse impacts;
- data flows, decision authority and power asymmetries;
- safeguards, less intrusive alternatives and residual risks;
- consultation or specialist review that occurred;
- tests and evidence supporting the safeguards; and
- the accountable owner, review date and conditions requiring reassessment.

Absence of a completed evaluation blocks a project claim that the relevant feature supports or preserves human
rights. It does not permit a default conclusion that no risk exists.

participating in an investigation.

## Enforcement

Project maintainers may remove content, reject changes, restrict access to project spaces, withdraw project
endorsement or support, and apply temporary or permanent participation bans. Responses should consider severity,
intent, repeated conduct, power imbalance, risk of continuing harm, willingness to remediate and the needs of affected
people.

Serious or imminent threats to safety may require prompt protective action before a full review. Outcomes should be
documented without unnecessarily identifying affected people. Where project technology caused or contributed to
harm, maintainers should also consider technical correction, notification, mitigation, remedy and prevention of
recurrence rather than treating the matter only as interpersonal misconduct.

## Relationship to the software license

This Code of Conduct governs participation in CSS – Databox project spaces and the uses the project will endorse or
support. The current MIT License permits broad use, modification and distribution and this document does not silently
replace or narrow those legal permissions.

If a legally binding human-rights condition on all use or redistribution is desired, that requires an explicit and
separately reviewed licensing or contractual decision. Such a restriction would no longer be the standard MIT License
and may affect whether the software qualifies as open source. Regardless of that legal boundary, respect for human
rights remains a core purpose and a condition of participation in, endorsement by and support from this project.
